Understanding the Binomial Distribution

The Binomial distribution models the number of successes in a sequence of independent Bernoulli trials, each with the same probability of success.

Problem Statement

Calculate the probability of achieving exactly k successes in n independent Bernoulli trials, each with probability p of success, using the Binomial distribution formula.

Parameters:

  • n: Total number of trials
  • k: Number of successes
  • p: Probability of success on each trial

Return: Probability of k successes in n trials

Mathematical Formulation

The probability of exactly k successes in n trials is calculated using the Binomial formula: \[ P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1 - p)^{n - k} \] where:
  • \(\binom{n}{k}\) is the binomial coefficient, calculated as n! / (k!(n - k)!)
  • p is the probability of success in a single trial
  • (1 - p) is the probability of failure

Implementation Steps

  1. Calculate the binomial coefficient \(\binom{n}{k}\).
  2. Compute the probability using the binomial formula.
  3. Return the calculated probability, rounded to five decimal places.

Example Calculation

Suppose you have 10 trials, a target of 5 successes, and a 0.5 probability of success per trial. Then: \[ P(X = 5) = \binom{10}{5} (0.5)^5 (1 - 0.5)^{10 - 5} = 0.24609 \]

Applications

The Binomial distribution is widely used to model situations where outcomes are binary (e.g., success/failure, yes/no), such as:
  • Predicting the probability of a certain number of positive responses in a survey.
  • Estimating the probability of a certain number of defective items in a batch.
  • Modeling financial scenarios with success or failure outcomes.
